# Spreadsheet Moment - Target Audience Personas

## Persona Development Framework

These personas represent the core user segments for Spreadsheet Moment. They are based on research, market analysis, and user interviews from the competitive landscape.

## Primary Personas

### Persona 1: Dr. Sarah Chen - The Research Scientist

**Demographics:**
- **Age:** 34
- **Location:** San Francisco, CA
- **Education:** PhD in Computational Biology, Stanford
- **Occupation:** Postdoctoral Researcher, UCSF
- **Income:** $75,000/year

**Psychographics:**
- **Values:** Reproducibility, transparency, collaboration
- **Personality:** Analytical, detail-oriented, collaborative

- **Learning Style:** Hands-on experimentation, documentation-driven

**Goals:**

2. Collaborate with researchers across institutions
3. Process large genomic datasets efficiently
4. Share findings with both technical and non-technical audiences

**Pain Points:**
- "Jupyter notebooks are great for exploration, but terrible for collaboration and version control"

**Messaging That Resonates:**

## Persona Development Process

### Research Methods Used
1. **Competitor Analysis** - Reviewed user personas from Excel, Google Sheets, Jupyter, Tableau
2. **Market Research** - Analyzed spreadsheet and computational tool user surveys
3. **Interview Synthesis** - Extracted insights from existing user interviews
4. **Team Expertise** - Leveraged team experience with similar user bases

### Validation Approach
1. **User Interviews** - Conduct 20+ interviews with each persona type
2. **Survey Research** - Survey broader user base to validate assumptions
3. **A/B Testing** - Test messaging with different persona segments
4. **Usage Analytics** - Analyze how different users interact with the product

### Iteration Process
1. **Initial Persona Development** - Create draft personas based on research
2. **Validation Interviews** - Test assumptions with real users
3. **Refinement** - Update personas based on feedback
4. **Ongoing Updates** - Quarterly persona reviews and updates

## Anti-Personas (Who We're Not For)

### Anti-Persona 1: The Casual Spreadsheet User
**Who:** Someone who uses spreadsheets occasionally for simple lists and basic calculations

**Why Not For Them:**
- Spreadsheet Moment is overkill for their needs
- They don't need advanced computational capabilities
- Free alternatives like Google Sheets meet their needs

**Recommended Alternative:** Google Sheets, Excel Online

### Anti-Persona 2: The Pure Coder
**Who:** Someone who exclusively uses code (Python, R, etc.) and refuses to use graphical interfaces

**Why Not For Them:**
- They prefer code-based workflows
- They don't see value in spreadsheet interfaces
- They've built custom tools that work for them

**Recommended Alternative:** Jupyter, VS Code, custom development

### Anti-Persona 3: The Enterprise Traditionalist
**Who:** Someone in a highly regulated enterprise that requires approved vendor software and extensive compliance processes

**Why Not For Them (Yet):**
- Spreadsheet Moment is too new for their compliance requirements
- They need enterprise-grade support and SLAs
- Their organization resists open-source tools

**Recommended Alternative:** Excel with enterprise add-ons, SAS, MATLAB

## Conclusion

These personas guide our product development, marketing, sales, and customer success efforts. They help us stay focused on solving real problems for real users, rather than building features we think are cool.

**Remember:** Personas are tools, not rules. Real people are more complex than any persona can capture. Use these as a starting point for understanding, but always listen to individual user needs and contexts.
